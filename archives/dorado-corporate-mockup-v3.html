The gap we close

Companies don't have a hard time abroad because their product is weak. They struggle because everything they're good at — the product, the pricing, the way they sell — was refined for a market they already understand. What's missing in a new market is never the product. It's the other side of the deal: who buys, why now, through whom, and in what package. That understanding is the gap. Closing it is what we do.

The other side

The buyer decided
before you arrived.

In Japan — and in most mature B2B markets — the evaluation starts long before the first meeting. Buyers screen you through your public information, your materials, and signals you don't know you're sending. If you only prepare your own side, you're optimizing for a conversation that may never happen.

Find

How they find you

Procurement teams search, longlist, and filter suppliers on their own terms: registries, platforms, exhibitions, referrals. We map the actual entry routes into a target buyer — not generic "business culture" advice.

Judge

How they judge you

Before anyone replies, you've been checked: credibility signals, quality systems, certifications, whether your information can survive being forwarded to a boss. We identify exactly what's missing from the buyer's point of view.

Decide

How they decide

A champion at a Japanese buyer doesn't decide alone. They carry your case into internal review. We structure what you give them so it can travel through that process — specs, proof, and a reason to pick you now.

No playbook? For a genuinely new category, or a niche buyer who has never evaluated something like this before, there are no existing criteria to map — for either side. There, the work flips: we go deep into the buyer's business first — what they actually do, what constrains them, who decides — and help build the case from the ground up.

What we actually do

Interest was never
your problem.

Meetings happen. Demos go well. Free trials, pilot talks, "let's explore a partnership." For most companies entering Japan, interest is easy — and none of it converts. The real work is everything after interest. Here is what that looks like in a live engagement:

01

Diagnose the pipeline honestly

We audit every open opportunity and grade the whole funnel — then hand you a deal-by-deal priority list: which accounts to push, which to nurture, and which to stop investing in entirely. Telling you what to stop doing is half the value.

02

Find where the demand actually is

Before anyone sells anything, we research candidate segments in depth — a dozen or more when needed. For each: does this segment really have the problem, who owns it internally, what volume flows through it, and can it pay?

03

Test ideas against evidence — including yours

When you propose a positioning, we score it, not applaud it. Some ideas come back "technically sound, commercially weak — here's why." Clients pay us to be right, not agreeable.

04

Put numbers on it first

Market sizing, volume estimates per prospect, revenue models per scenario — built before outreach, so effort goes where the economics work.

05

Run the outreach and keep deals warm

We send the messages, track every reply, and never let warm interest expire from neglect. Every contact has a next action with a date — our own system won't let us close a lead without one.

06

Stand in the room

We sit in the live conversations, understand both your technology and the buyer's internal approval process, and turn every interaction into a documented, reusable asset — so the quality doesn't depend on your founder being present.

Method

Market development,
run like an engineering system.

Go-to-market work fails quietly: messages go out, meetings happen, and a year later nobody can say what worked or why. We built our own operating system to make that impossible. Every hypothesis, every message, every reply, and every dead end is recorded, measured, and turned into a reusable asset.

LANE

One question

Every engagement is organized around testable questions — like "will mid-size Japanese manufacturers buy this component from a foreign second source?"

BATCH

One test

Each question is tested through batches of real companies: who, angle, channel, planned volume. No batch is allowed to just fade out.

CALL

One decision

Every batch ends with an explicit call on what happens next — recorded, with the evidence that produced it.

One structure for everything

Company→ Contact→ Touch history→ Status→ Next action

Each active account has a standing dossier you can read in sixty seconds — including, in bold, whose ball it is right now. Nothing depends on anyone's memory. If a project pauses for three months, it resumes from the file, not from recollection.

Measured

Messages are measured like product features

Every message is archived verbatim with its metadata: the hook, the template, whether it got a reply. A draft only becomes a reusable template after it has demonstrably worked — and the library records what failed, too.

Exit-ready

The exit is designed in from day one

The end state of our work is not dependence on Dorado. When an account is qualified — real problem, real owner, real timeline — it is packaged and transferred into your own CRM: contacts, channels, research, and the full touch history. You own the relationship. The playbook stays with you.

One example from the log: hooks built on the prospect's own situation — a third-party review of their product, a change in their stack — earn same-day replies. Generic industry news earns silence. We know this because we measured it.

None of this is theoretical. The system described here is the one this page was written from — the same files, the same database, the same rules we apply to client work every day. When we say we'll give you a clear call on a market, this is the machinery that produces it.

How we operate with you

Honest calls,
not comfortable ones.

Some of the most valuable sentences we deliver sound like this — said early, with the evidence attached:

Said to a client · 01
“This deal is a nurture, not a pipeline item.”

Said when a forecast is carrying deals that will never close this year. Naming them frees the team to work the ones that will.

Said to a client · 02
“Free pilots are increasing your workload, not your revenue.”

Said when goodwill is being mistaken for demand. Interest that costs you money every month is not traction.

Said to a client · 03
“This partner is structurally unable to sell for you.”

Said when a channel looks busy but cannot close. Loyalty to a broken structure is one of the most expensive habits in market entry.

Principle 01

Start from the next decision

We scope work around the decision you actually need to make next — not the broadest possible engagement we could sell you.

Principle 02

Transparent about uncertainty

Facts, hypotheses, and to-verify items are labeled as what they are. We don't dress up unknowns as solved.

Principle 03

Validate before scaling

Hiring, local entities, and big campaigns come after the path is tested — never before, on optimism.

Principle 04

With you, not around you

We push back, ask questions, and show our reasoning rather than executing blindly on instructions.

The three things restaurants check before taste when considering a new ingredient.

A foreign food producer assumed the tasting would decide everything. The buyer's kitchen had already decided three things before the sample was ever opened. Here is what they were — and what that means for how you approach Japanese restaurants.

2026.07 7 min read By Dorado
dorado-group.jp/resources/use-cases
Image slot · 16:10

Dummy intro copy. A producer with a genuinely good product kept losing deals after well-received tastings. When we mapped the buyer's side of the decision, the pattern was clear: taste was the last gate, not the first. Three checks came before it.

The situation Placeholder summary. A foreign ingredient maker, strong reviews at trade shows, samples requested by dozens of Tokyo restaurants — and almost no repeat orders. The question we were brought: is the product wrong, or is the approach wrong?

01Can the kitchen actually use it?

Dummy paragraph. Before anyone tastes anything, the chef checks operational fit: prep time, storage, shelf life once opened, and whether it slots into dishes already on the menu without retraining staff. An ingredient that demands a new workflow is judged as a cost, not a flavor.

  • Storage: dummy line about refrigeration and space constraints in small kitchens.
  • Portioning: dummy line about pack sizes that don't match service volume.
  • Consistency: dummy line about batch-to-batch variation being a deal-breaker.

02Will supply survive success?

Dummy paragraph. The second check is continuity: if the dish becomes a signature, can you deliver every week, at the same grade, through Japanese import channels? Restaurants have been burned before. A great ingredient that disappears for six weeks damages the menu it was supposed to elevate.

The tasting doesn't sell the ingredient. It confirms a decision the kitchen has already half-made.

03Can the story be told at the table?

Dummy paragraph. The third check is whether the ingredient gives the restaurant something to say — an origin, a maker, a reason — that staff can explain to a guest in one sentence. This is where foreign producers underinvest: the materials exist for importers, not for the person holding the menu.

04What we did with this

Dummy paragraph. We rebuilt the approach in the buyer's order: operational one-pager first, supply commitment second, story card third, tasting last. Outreach volume stayed the same; the sequence changed.

The call

Placeholder outcome. The product was never the problem. The sequence was. Repeat-order conversations opened at the same tasting count — the difference was what arrived before the sample did.

What this means if you sell to Japanese restaurants

✓
Lead with operations, not flavor. Dummy line: answer the kitchen's first question before they ask it.
✓
Prove continuity early. Dummy line: name your import route and your restock cadence in the first materials.
✓
Arm the table, not just the buyer. Dummy line: give staff one sentence they can say to a guest.

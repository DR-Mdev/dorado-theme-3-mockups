Reading the market

Japan reads differently.
So the work is built differently.

Three patterns show up in almost every engagement here. They explain most of what looks, from the outside, like hesitation.

01

Specialists do specialist work

Japan entry has a predictable failure pattern: the one person who happens to speak Japanese becomes the de facto negotiator, translator, and relationship-builder — whether or not that was ever their job. It feels efficient early. It rarely scales. Language fluency and sales fluency are different skills, even when they live in the same person. Our work is putting the right function in the right seat.

02

Trust compounds. So does the lack of it.

What you say in Japan becomes something close to an unwritten contract. Partners take commitments literally and remember exactly when they weren't kept. Relationships move slowly by design, not by accident: before anyone recommends you internally, they want to know who they are working with — because their own reputation rides on it.

03

Proof isn't a formality. It's the argument.

Japanese companies rarely move on a pitch alone, however compelling. What convinces them is evidence that something has already worked — with a comparable customer, in a comparable situation. So the real job of Japan go-to-market isn't introductions. It's building that proof, case by case, hypothesis by hypothesis.

The response — how every engagement runs

HYPOTHESIS

Make it explicit

Every engagement starts as a falsifiable claim about the market — not a vague ambition to "do Japan".

PROBE

Test it small

Real accounts, real outreach, real replies. Small enough to be cheap to be wrong about.

EVIDENCE

Build the proof

Each cycle leaves material a champion can take to their own team — the thing that actually moves decisions here.

The bet Slower to look impressive on day one. The only version of fast that actually holds up in this market.

How we work

Operating commitments,
not aspirations.

Six principles that shape every engagement. They are not values on a wall — they determine how Dorado behaves across every client relationship.

01

Work from the next decision that matters

We start by identifying what actually needs to be clarified or confirmed next — not by building the broadest possible scope from day one.

02

Structured progress, not vague activity

Progress means visible movement on the right things: active priorities, shared updates, and clear next steps. Volume of work is not the metric.

03

Prioritize realistic sequencing

Not everything should happen at the same time. What gets started, deferred, or re-ordered is often the most important decision of all.

04

Be transparent about uncertainty

Japan entry involves real unknowns. We explain what we can and cannot know, and never dress up open questions as solved problems.

05

Avoid inflated promises

We can improve clarity, speed, and coordination. We cannot honestly guarantee revenue, meetings, or timelines in a new market — so we don't.

06

Work with clients, not around them

The best outcomes come from shared thinking and shared ownership. We push back, ask questions, and flag issues rather than executing blindly.

Our posture

Operating within
what is realistic.

Dorado works on a reasonable-efforts basis: serious, genuinely committed work — and honesty that Japan entry outcomes depend on many factors outside any external partner's control.

P · 01

Market uncertainty is real

Japan entry involves genuine unknowns. We help reduce that uncertainty — we do not pretend it does not exist.

P · 02

Results depend on more than support quality

Product fit, pricing, timing, internal capacity, and market conditions all affect outcomes beyond any external partner's influence.

P · 03

Speed and quality both require input

Work moves faster and lands better when the client is actively sharing context, decisions, and access in a timely way.

P · 04

Scope should reflect the current decision

The right starting scope is often smaller than companies expect. Starting lean and expanding as confidence grows is usually the better path.

P · 05

Iteration is part of the process

Hypotheses about Japan will need to be updated. That is not failure — that is how market entry learning actually works.

Dorado view The most useful thing we can do: help companies ask better questions, make better decisions, and move at a pace that keeps the work coherent — without unnecessary risk or cost.

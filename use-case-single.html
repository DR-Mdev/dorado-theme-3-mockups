01 Validate Fleet & telematics SaaS ~3 months

Validating Japan market entry for a fleet-telematics SaaS provider, in three months, with evidence instead of assumptions

A fleet-telematics SaaS company with hundreds of deployments abroad wanted to enter Japan. We designed and executed a hypothesis-driven research program, and turned the findings into a validated go-to-market strategy and sales-ready Japanese materials.

Dorado

Client case, anonymized · Tokyo

Note on naming An NDA is in place for this engagement. The client is referred to anonymously below. Before publishing with a real name, written approval from the client is required.

The Challenge

The client had built a successful fleet-telematics business abroad, where regulatory reporting requirements had made digital adoption the norm. Japan looked like a natural next market: a large trucking sector, tightening labor regulation, and carriers under pressure to modernize.

But Japan is a different world: strict driver working-hour caps instead of the client's home-market rules, a fragmented multi-tier subcontracting structure, entrenched domestic platforms, and long-term contracts that make displacing an installed dispatch system difficult. The client needed to know, before committing resources: is there a viable entry point, and what would it take to win it?

What We Did

We structured the engagement as a research program with defined workstreams, weekly progress reviews, and a deliverable tracker shared with the client.

Market analysis

We sized the Japanese fleet-telematics market against global benchmarks, built growth forecasts under multiple CAGR scenarios, and mapped pricing across the market, including hardware pricing, bundled subscription tiers, and installation-and-maintenance options that are unique to Japanese fleet operators.

Competitive analysis

We produced SWOT analyses of the leading domestic platforms, function-by-function feature comparison matrices, pricing comparison tables, and sales battle cards. To understand how these products actually perform in the field, we mined and synthesized dozens of published user reviews, surfacing recurring complaints (support quality, hidden costs, integration friction with existing dispatch software) that became attack points for the client's positioning.

Regulatory and cultural research

We mapped Japan's revised driver working-hour standards (tightened in 2024), the state of digital-tachograph adoption, and the differences between the client's home-market rules and Japan's logistics regulatory regime, including the multi-tier subcontracting structure that foreign entrants routinely underestimate. We also assessed the impact of the government's logistics-digitalization subsidy program on carrier purchasing behavior.

Hypothesis-driven market validation

Rather than a generic market report, we worked with the client to articulate explicit strategic hypotheses and test each one against evidence:

  • Regional carriers are underserved white space, while major logistics hubs are locked up by incumbents. Direct telematics adoption data by region did not exist, so we built a proxy model from government transport statistics (MLIT data, regional freight surveys) to estimate regional adoption gaps.
  • Non-adoption has identifiable, addressable causes. Research confirmed the barriers: driver pushback against monitoring (the top-cited reason in operator surveys, strongest among owner-operators), cost-benefit doubts, integration concerns with legacy dispatch software, and reliance on informal subcontractor relationships in regional trucking.

The output was not just data but a decision: pursue regional white space rather than head-to-head displacement at major logistics hubs, with each supporting assumption documented and sourced.

GTM strategy and localization

We identified a differentiated wedge: regulatory compliance. Regional logistics associations and prefectural governments were actively pushing carriers toward digital compliance tools, and the client's platform was purpose-built for exactly that requirement. We built the go-to-market strategy around partnerships with regional logistics associations, equipment resellers, and system integrators, drafted the Japanese sales deck around a concrete model case, localized customer success stories from the client's home market, and converted pricing into Japan-ready plans. We also prepared reseller and end-user target lists for outreach.

Hypotheses first, research second. Every research task existed to confirm or kill a specific strategic assumption, nothing was researched "for completeness."

The Outcome

In roughly three months, the client went from an open question ("should we enter Japan?") to:

  • A sourced, scenario-based view of market size and growth
  • A validated beachhead strategy (regional carriers, compliance-driven adoption) with the reasoning behind each rejected alternative documented
  • Competitor battle cards and positioning built on real user pain points
  • A complete Japanese-language sales deck and pricing structure, ready for first customer conversations
  • Named target lists of resellers and end users to begin market validation outreach

Why It Worked

Hypotheses first, research second. Every research task existed to confirm or kill a specific strategic assumption, nothing was researched "for completeness."

Proxy modeling where data doesn't exist. When direct adoption data was unavailable, we built defensible estimates from public government statistics instead of stopping at "no data."

Local nuance foreign entrants miss. Working-hour compliance, subcontracting structures, subsidy dynamics, and regional integrator ecosystems were treated as first-class strategic inputs, not footnotes.

Research that ends in sales tools. Findings flowed directly into battle cards, a localized deck, and target lists, assets the client could use the week the engagement ended.

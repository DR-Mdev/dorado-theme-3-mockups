No formula survives
contact with Japan.

Your product, your buyer, the channel, the timing: every market entry is a different equation with different variables. Anyone selling you a step-by-step formula is selling reassurance. Why show you this

Go-to-market work fails quietly: messages go out, meetings happen, and a year later nobody can say what worked or why. With this many unknowns, the honest edge isn't a secret playbook, it's being able to say, at any moment, what was tested and what was learned. We built our own operating system to make that the default.

Adjectives vs. tables Most agencies describe how they work in adjectives: "hands-on," "data-driven," "local expertise." Ours can be described in database tables. Every claim on this page corresponds to a system we run daily, not a slide.

One structure for everything

Nothing depends on anyone's memory.

Underneath every engagement (whatever the product, whatever the stage) runs the same spine. The structured backbone lives in a database; the depth (research memos, message archives, account dossiers) lives in versioned files, linked to it.

The spine

Company→ Contact→ Touch history→ Status→ Next action

If a project pauses for three months, it resumes from the file, not from recollection.

Every account has a standing dossier

A STATUS file you can read in sixty seconds (including, in bold, whose ball it is right now), a cross-channel timeline, a verbatim archive of every message sent, an untouched raw log, and dated research snapshots.

One file to know the state; the rest is evidence.

How an engagement unfolds

Three jobs, not three products.

Deciding whether to enter, turning interest into revenue, and taking care of the customers you win are different kinds of work, with different rhythms and different deliverables. Every engagement moves through some or all of these three stages, running on the same spine above.

01 Validate

“Should we enter Japan at all?”

Market research & entry validation. You have a hypothesis and no local evidence yet. We run structured research sprints that turn "we think Japan could work" into a prioritized, evidence-backed entry plan.

02 Sell

“Everyone is interested. Nobody is buying.”

Go-to-market operations. Meetings happen, demos go well, and none of it converts. We run the commercial layer — pipeline, outreach, positioning — as the lane / batch / call system described below.

03 Operate

“We won our first Japanese customer. We have nobody in Japan.”

Customer success representation. The deal is signed; someone has to sit in the meetings, walk the site, and keep the relationship alive, in Japanese. We act as your embedded team on the ground.

Priced as Performance or Retainer plans on Services; proof of each from real engagements on Use cases.

Hypotheses, not hunches

Every thesis is written down
so it can be proven wrong.

Because the variables differ for every company, we don't start from a recipe, we start from a falsifiable claim: "companies of this profile, under this trigger, with this buyer, will respond." Then we test it against real companies, in fixed batches, with response measured per company, not per anecdote.

LANE

One question

A market thesis phrased so it can be true or false, not a segment name, a question. Each lane exists to get that question answered.

BATCH

One test

A fixed cohort of real companies: who, angle, channel, planned volume. A pilot batch first, then validation cohorts of hundreds.

CALL

One decision

No batch is allowed to just fade out. Every one ends with an explicit call, recorded with the evidence that produced it.

Continue

The evidence supports the thesis: same conditions, next batch.

Adjust

The thesis survives in a modified form: conditions change, the evidence carries over.

Fork

The buyer or the buying motive changed: that's a new question, and the evidence counter resets. We don't quietly blend two theses into one.

Stop spending

The thesis is dead. Killing it fast is a result, not a failure: it's knowledge you no longer have to pay for.

Hard rule Metrics are never typed in by hand. Reply rates, contact counts, and last-activity dates are derived from the event log itself. If a number is on our dashboard, there's a recorded event behind it. And exploratory pilots are never extrapolated into conclusions: that's a rule, not a habit.

Three tracks

Not all outreach is the same work.

Cold list work, warm follow-up, and strategic account pursuit are different jobs with different rhythms. Forcing them into one pipeline is how CRMs turn into graveyards. We run them as three explicit tracks: every company in the system is in exactly one.

LIST

The cold pool

High-rotation qualification of researched companies. The goal is not volume of messages, it's a fast, evidence-based sort: who is worth pursuing, who isn't, and why.

FOLLOW-UP

Person-level, warm

The moment someone replies, the unit of work changes from company to person. Each warm contact gets a next action with a date: our queue won't let a lead be closed without one. Warm interest is never allowed to expire from neglect.

ABM

Account-level, strategic

Some accounts justify pursuit before anyone replies. We map the key people and track coverage (who has been reached, who has responded, where the gaps are), reviewed weekly against the actual contact log, not against optimism.

The message library

Messages are measured
like product features.

Templates earn their place

Every message we send is archived verbatim with its metadata: what the hook was, which template it came from, whether it got a reply, and how effective it proved. A draft only becomes a reusable template after it has demonstrably worked, and the library records what failed, too.

One example from the log: hooks built on the prospect's own situation (a third-party review of their product, a change in their stack) earn same-day replies. Generic industry news earns silence. We know this because we measured it, and every future message benefits from it.

The handoff

The exit is designed in
from day one.

You own the relationship

The end state of our work is not dependence on Dorado. When an account is qualified (real problem, real owner, real timeline), it is packaged through standard handoff templates and transferred into your own CRM: contacts, channels, triggers, research, and the full touch history.

The client owns the relationship. The playbook stays with them.

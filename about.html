Not sector-specific, proven

Four sectors that share
nothing except the problem.

Industrial robotics, logistics software, food products, and vertical software have almost nothing in common as products. What they had in common as clients was the same missing piece: a structured read on the Japanese buyer's side of the deal.

Industrial automation

Machine vision & line robotics

Machine-vision inspection and line automation for factories. Client-facing work required fluency in PLCs, conveyors, and vision systems, in two languages, without losing precision.

Logistics software

Fleet & telematics SaaS

Hundreds of fleet deployments abroad, zero in Japan. The research had to account for a different regulatory regime, strict driver-hour rules, and keiretsu-bound logistics incumbents.

Food & beverage

Food, a broad product range

A range with no fixed Japan strategy yet, not even a settled channel. The research had to decide the product, the channel, and the positioning at once.

Vertical SaaS

Industry-specific software platform

A technically superior product competing with both incumbent platforms and general-purpose alternatives. The constraint was never the technology: it was the commercial layer around it.

Where this comes from

We didn't arrive at this idea in the abstract. It showed up independently on a factory floor, in a logistics-software company's board deck, and in a software company's stalled pipeline: what's missing is never the product. It's the other side of the deal: who buys, why now, through whom, and in what package. Four different engagements kept teaching us the same lesson, which is the only reason we now say it with confidence.

Why this became the company Dorado exists because that gap kept showing up regardless of sector, product maturity, or company size, and because closing it is research work, not a translation job or a sales favor.

In their own engagement records

Three lines pulled straight
from the case files.

Not testimonials, excerpts from the actual deliverables and engagement notes, kept as written.

Logistics-software provider · research memo
“When direct adoption data didn't exist, we built a defensible estimate from public statistics, we didn't stop at ‘no data.’”

Public demand data for the client's category simply wasn't published. Rather than treat that as a dead end, we built a proxy model from public government statistics.

Food brand · research memo
“The research surfaced findings that materially changed the plan, including ones the client didn't expect to hear.”

Consumer data showed the biggest barrier wasn't price or product: it was that Japanese shoppers bought the category through a channel the brand hadn't planned for. The strategy was rebuilt around that fact.

Vertical SaaS company · GTM operations note
“We treat every signup as a research problem before it becomes an email.”

Standing operating rule for inbound follow-up: company scan, buying-context hypothesis, then a tailored message, in that order, every time, never the reverse.
